Why this exists

Your home was designed for someone else.

If you have ADHD, autism, sensory processing differences, or a neurodivergent family — or if you’re simply a burnt-out parent whose home constantly works against you — you already know that standard home organization advice doesn’t work.

Not because you’re not trying hard enough. Because the advice was written for a different nervous system.

“The pile isn’t the problem. The pile is your nervous system finding the path of least resistance in a space that offers no better option.”

Traditional home design starts with the mood board. The aesthetic. The Pinterest image of a space that photographs beautifully for someone who doesn’t actually live in it.

The Neuro Aesthetic starts at the bottom. With your nervous system. With the neuroscience of how your brain actually processes your environment. With safety, regulation, and function — before a single bin is purchased or shelf is installed.

The neuroscience behind this: Within 150 milliseconds of entering a space, your brain has already decided whether it’s safe. Before you’ve thought a single conscious thought about it, your nervous system has filed a report.

For ADHD and autistic brains, those reports are louder, more urgent, and more consequential than conventional design has ever acknowledged. A space that works against your nervous system doesn’t just feel uncomfortable. It costs you spoons — every single day — before you’ve done anything else.

The Neuro Aesthetic is the first course built around this science, applied to the home you actually live in.

Who built this

Built by an autistic architect who needed it herself.

I’m Jessica Weiss — a late-diagnosed autistic/AuDHD architect, interior designer, and parent of two neurodivergent children with sensory processing differences. For years I tried to make my home work. I read all the books. I tried all the systems. And the pile kept coming back.

Then I got my diagnosis. And everything clicked. The problem wasn’t me. It was that every system I’d ever tried was built for a neurotypical brain. And I wasn’t one.

I designed The Sage Sanctuary — a sensory-friendly short-term rental in Denver — as proof of concept. Every decision started with one question: what does this space need to do for the body inside it? The result: a home rated in the top 10% of properties on Airbnb, where guests regularly say they don’t know what it is about this place — but they feel different here.
